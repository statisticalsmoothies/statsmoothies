Flexible parametric models for dose-response analysis in miRNAs expression

Giacomo Biganzoli1, 2, Giuseppe Marano1, Chiara Favero3, Valentina Bollati3, Joel Schwartz2, Patrizia Boracchi1

1 Department of Biomedical and Clinical Sciences, University of Milan, Milan, Italy; 2 Department of Environmental Health, Harvard T.H.Chan School of Public Health, Boston, MA; 3 Department of Clinical and Community Health Sciences, University of Milan, Milan, Italy

Dose-response analysis of miRNA expression

Analyzing how the expression of microRNAs (miRNAs) changes in response to varying levels of air pollution is a critical tool for environmental health. This approach allows scientists and public health officials to understand the molecular underpinnings of how pollutants impact human health. Just as with a drug, the “dose” of a pollutant can determine the severity and nature of the biological response.

Motivating example

The data we will use comes from the SPHERE study (“Susceptibility to Particle Health Effects, miRNAs and Exosomes”), a cross-sectional study that also investigated the effects of exposure to particulate matter with an aerodynamic diameter ≤10 μm (PM10) on extracellular vescicles (EVs) and their miRNA content in overweight and obese individuals. In particular, the research of Pergoli et al. (2017) aimed to determine if short-term PM10 exposure was associated with changes in fibrinogen levels, the release of EVs, and the miRNA content within those EVs. The study also explored how these molecular changes might be linked to increased coagulation and cardiovascular disease risk.

In the study, hsa-let-7c-5p was one of nine EVs-packaged miRNAs that were found to be down-regulated in response to short-term exposure to PM10. We will focus our attention to that specific miRNA to explore the distributional changes associated to PM10 exposure.

Non-normally distributed outcomes

Figure 1: Different scenarios that describe the impact of the exposure on the distribution of an outcome

Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) and Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) are primarily designed to model the relationship between an exposure and the conditional expectation of an outcome variable. For example, a linear model expressed asE[expression | PM10] = β₀ + β₁ * PM10 specifically quantifies how the average level of miRNA expression changes with a unit increase in PM10 exposure. This approach is most effective when the predictor induces a simple location shift in the outcome’s distribution, a scenario often assumed under normality (as depicted in Figure 1, panel A).

However, higher exposure levels could lead to both a lower location of the distribution and reduced variability around that location (Figure 1, panel B). In other cases, an exposure might only impact the variance while leaving the location unchanged (Figure 1, panel C). A model focused solely on the location would erroneously conclude there is no association in the latter case. Furthermore, if an outcome’s distribution is bimodal (or multimodal) its mean could fall in a low-density region between the peaks. Modeling this single, unrepresentative value would completely obscure the underlying nature of the relationship.

Modeling the full (conditional) distribution of the outcome

We propose a framework for analyzing the effect of exposure factors like air-pollution on the full distribution of an outcome. We exploit flexible parametric models that adopt splines to flexibly and robustly estimate two fundamental functions that describe a distribution, the cumulative distribution function (\(\small F(y)\), CDF) and the Probability Density Function ( \(\small f(y)\), PDF). We choose Highest Density Regions (HDRs) (Hyndman 1996) to easily communicate the association between exposure and outcome, translating the possibly complex model results into easily interpretable probabilistic statements.

The flexible models adopted to estimate \(\small f(y)\) and \(\small F(y)\) are of the form:

\(\small g[Y] = \beta X + \sum_{k=1}^{K} (\gamma_k + \delta_k X) B_k(y)\)

where \(\small Y\) is either the hazard function \(\small h(y)\) or the cumulative distribution function \(\small F(y)\) of the outcome variable; \(\small \beta X\) is the effect associated to the exposure covariate on \(\small Y\); \(\small B_k(y)\) are the spline bases to smooth the baseline \(\small Y\); \(\small \gamma_k\) are the coefficients associated to the spline bases; \(\small \delta_kX\) are the possible interaction effects between the baseline function and the exposure covariate \(\small X\) on the CDF; \(\small g(\cdot)\) is a suitable link function that links the response variable \(\small Y\) to the linear predictor.

For \(\small F(y)\) we use jackknife pseudo-observations (PSEUDO) computed on the empirical CDF \(\small \hat{\theta}_i(y) = n \cdot \hat{F}(y) - (n-1) \cdot \hat{F}_{-i}(y)\). We obtain \(\small f(y)\) by the direct relationship \(\small f(y|X) = \frac{d}{dy} F(y|X)\).

From the hazard, we obtain \(\small f(y|X)\) with the relationship \(\small f(y|X) = h(y|X) \exp\left(-\int_{0}^{t} h(u|X) \, du\right)\), and \(\small F(y|X)\) with the relationship \(\small F(y|X) = 1- \exp\left(-\int_{0}^{t} h(u|X) \, du\right)\).

They are really flexible! A classical example

Figure 2: Estimates of the PDF and CDF adopting the PWE and the PSEUDO models. The estimates are compared with the ones from a Kernel Density Estimator and the empirical CDF.

A classic example of density estimation, featured on Wikipedia, uses a Kernel Density Estimator (KDE) to model the glucose distribution in the Indian PIMA population based on diabetes status. Our method matches the flexibility of KDE while adding the significant advantage of accounting for continuous covariates. In Figure 2, the KDE estimates of \(\small f(\text{glu}|\text{Diabetes})\) (white thin lines) are compared with the ones from one method, by performing smooth estimation either of the hazard with a Piecewise exponential model (PWE) (Laird and Olivier 1981) -panel A- or of the cumulative distribution function (PSEUDO) (Andersen 2003) -panel C. In panel B and D we show that the estimates of the CDF \(\small F(\text{glu}|\text{Diabetes})\) from both the modeling frameworks match well the empirical ones.

Interpretable statements about the outcome

The challenge with using flexible models lies in interpretation. Standard GLM coefficients, do not provide a sensible interpretation in this context (e.g., as hazard/cumulative distribution ratios or differences).

Figure 3: Different propability intervals to describe a distribution

On the other hand, relying solely on a visual plot of the density function provides qualitative insight but no quantitative measure of the effect. To address this, we use HDRs - the smallest regions containing a specific probability mass-, which translate the visual changes in a distribution’s shape into formal and interpretable probability statements about the outcome. In Figure 3C, the 75% Highest Density Region is the best suited, over intervals showed in panel A and B, for summarizing a right skewed distribution, as it contains the (75%) most representative values of it.

Two algorithms identify a distribution’s Highest Density Region. The first method (Up-down approach) operates on the probability density function to find all areas exceeding a density threshold, a process that can yield multiple intervals and is ideal for multimodal distributions. The second algorithm (Right-left approach) uses the CDF to solve for the single shortest interval containing the target probability, making it a more direct approach for unimodal distributions. That’s why we are interested in estimate both the PDF/CDF.

Demonstration

We considered a PWE model to model the impact of short-term PM10 exposure on hsa-let-7c-5p expression. We specified a non-linear effect of PM10, modeled with a restricted cubic spline, on the baseline hazard function. The baseline hazard was modeled with natural splines and 3 interior knots, chosen with the Bayesian Information Criterion. From the hazard estimates we obtained the conditional CDF (Figure 4, top panel) and PDF (Figure 4, bottom panel) using their relationship with the hazard function. We used a bootstrap approach to compute 95% Simultaneous Confidence Bands (Montiel Olea and Plagborg-Møller 2018) and to display the variability around the function estimates.

From the top panel, we have evidence of a substantial difference between the CDFs, conditional to distinct exposure levels of PM10. Interestingly, the corresponding PDFs in the bottom panel show that the effect is realized more on “accumulation” of the probability mass around the peak instead than on the shift of the location of the distribution, like a OLS would model

Figure 4: Results adopting the PWE model to model the CDF and PDF conditional on levels of exposure to PM10. Bootstrapped 95% Simultaneous confidence bands show the variability around the estimates.

We then computed the 25%, 50%, 75% and 90% HDRs, conditional on the values of PM10 exposure. For example, the 25% most representative values of expression of the miRNA lie between 0.18 and 0.29 at an exposure of \(\small 10 \mu g/m^3\), (\(\small\text{25% HDR}_{PM10=10} = [0.18, 0.29]\)) and between 0.16 and 0.23 at an exposure of \(\small50 \mu g/m^3\) (\(\small\text{25% HDR}_{PM10=50} = [16, 23]\)). The 75% most representative expression values lie between 0.10 and 0.57 at an exposure of \(\small10 \mu g/m^3\) and between 0.8 and 0.37 at an exposure of \(\small50 \mu g/m^3\) (\(\small \text{75% HDR}_{PM10=10} = [.10, 0.57]\) vs \(\small\text{75% HDR}_{PM10=50} = [0.8, 0.37]\)).

Conclusion

Flexible parametric models have been widely adopted for modeling functions like the CDF and the hazard function and for describing the effect of an exposure on the entire distribution of an outcome. However, the interpretation of their outputs is hampered, especially when baseline-varying effects are modeled and the outcome of interest is not a ‘time-to-event’. Our methods exploit the flexibility of these models but translate their outputs into interpretable probabilistic statements about the most representative values of the conditional distribution of the outcome by using HDRs. We believe this approach might be useful to describe exposure-outcome relationships that do not fit the rigid distributions of classical parametric models, which is often the case for miRNA expression data, as demonstrated in the application above.
