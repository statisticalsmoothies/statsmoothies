A Semi-Parametric Approach to Dose-Response Modeling

Estimating Full Outcome Distributions in Epidemiology

Research Group

1. The Importance of Dose-Response

Why does it matter? 🩺

  • Understanding dose-response relationships is fundamental to epidemiology and public health.
  • It provides crucial evidence for shaping policies to protect community health.
  • Observational research helps:
    • Identify high-risk populations.
    • Guide interventions to reduce harmful behaviors (e.g., smoking).
    • Promote beneficial ones (e.g., exercise).

2. Traditional Approach: A Case Study

Physical Activity (PA) Exposure and Metabolic Syndrome (MSy) Outcome.

  • Traditional Method: Model the presence/absence of MSy (a binary outcome) using logistic regression.
    • log(odds of MSy) = β₀ + β₁ * CRF
  • Limitations of this approach:
    1. Late-Stage Focus: MSy is an advanced stage of dysfunction; effects of PA might be clearer in earlier stages.
    2. Methodological Issues: Binary classification of a complex condition is problematic.
    3. Multifactorial Nature: MSy components (adiposity, glucose, etc.) might be affected differently by CRF.

3. Beyond Binary: Modeling Continuous Outcomes

A more refined approach is to model the individual components of MSy on their continuous scales.

  • Common Method: Use Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) or Generalized Linear Models (GLM) to model the expected value (mean) of the outcome.
    • E[Glucose | CRF] = β₀ + β₁ * CRF
  • Limitation: only tells us about the average response. What about the entire shape of the outcome’s distribution? Rigid assumptions about the distribution of the outcome conditional on the exposure.

6. Moving Beyond the Mean

To get a complete picture, we need to model the entire probability distribution of the outcome.

  • Quantile Regression: Models specific percentiles (e.g., the 25th, 50th, 75th) of the outcome. A step in the right direction, but doesn’t give the full picture.

  • Probability Density Function (PDF): Describes the likelihood of any given outcome value. This provides a complete view of uncertainty and individual variability.

  • The Challenge: How do we estimate the conditional PDF (the PDF of the outcome for a given exposure level)?

    • Parametric methods assume a fixed distribution (e.g., Normal, log-normal, exponential), which often doesn’t hold.
    • Non-parametric methods (like Kernel Density Estimation) are flexible but struggle with multiple covariates and limited data.

6. Foundations: Flexible modeling on the F(y) and h(y)

We model the entire probability distribution considering two approaches:

1. Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF), \[ F(y) = P(Y \le y) \]

Flexible model proposed: \[g[F(y)] = \delta^T b_{p} + \gamma^T X\]

  • \(\delta^T b_{p}\) the baseline CDF smoothed with regression splines;

  • \(\gamma^T X\) effect of the exposure covariate \(X\) on the CDF;

  • \(g(\cdot)\) is a suitable link function that links the response variable \(F(y)\) to the linear predictor.

For \(F(y)\) we use jackknife pseudo-observations \(\hat{\theta}_i(y) = n \cdot \hat{F}(y) - (n-1) \cdot \hat{F}_{-i}(y)\).

6. Foundations: Flexible modeling on the F(y) and h(y)

2. Hazard function (h): \[h(y) = \lim_{\Delta y \to 0} \frac{P(y \le Y < y + \Delta y \mid Y \ge y)}{\Delta y}\]

The model is on a discretized version of the hazard: \[\lambda_{ij} = \lambda_i \exp(X_j' \gamma)\]

  • \(\lambda_{ij}\) represents the discrete hazard rate of \(Y\) in an interval \(i\) for subject \(j\)
  • \(\lambda_i\) represents the baseline discrete hazard (smoothed with splines) in the intervals \(I\)
  • \(\exp(X_j' \gamma)\) represents the effect of the exposure covariate \(X\) on the discrete hazard of \(Y\).

This leads to the log-linear model (piecewise exponential model) that can be fitted within the GLM framework:

\[ \log(\mu_{ij}) = \log(\tau_{ij}) + \log(\lambda_i) + X_j' \gamma \]

7. Our Proposed Semi-Parametric Method

8. Application 1: Simulation Study

Objective: Validate the method in a controlled environment.

  • Scenario: Analyze how Cardiorespiratory Fitness (CRF) influences the full distributions of fasting glucose and triglycerides.
  • Process:
    1. Simulate data where the true conditional PDFs are known.
    2. Apply our method to estimate the conditional PDFs and percentiles.
    3. Compare our estimates to the true known values to assess accuracy.
    4. Demonstrate computation of the joint PDF and CDF of glucose and triglycerides as a a function of CRF.

8. Application 2: Real-World Data

Objective: Demonstrate the method’s utility in a real epidemiological context.

  • Dataset: The Pima Indian Diabetes Study, a landmark study on type 2 diabetes risk factors.
  • Analysis: Explore the impact of adiposity (BMI) on the distributions of two key metabolic outcomes:
    1. Fasting Glucose
    2. Diastolic Blood Pressure
  • This showcases how the method can uncover nuanced relationships in real, complex data.

9. Conclusion & Impact

What does this new approach offer? 🚀

  • Goes Beyond Averages: It moves past simple mean-based regression to capture the entire distributional shift of a health outcome in response to an exposure.
  • Flexibility: The semi-parametric nature avoids rigid distributional assumptions.
  • Interpretability: Stays within the well-understood GLM/GAM framework.
  • Richer Insights: Provides a more complete and nuanced understanding of risk, which is vital for informing effective public health policy and evidence-based interventions.
